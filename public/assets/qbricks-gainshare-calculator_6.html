The evidence loop — why a client can trust the numbers

  1. Engage with the 5×5 (5 days)Five days, hands-on with the client's own estate: the use case is selected, the savings opportunity sized and the baseline approach scoped — so the POC starts with a measurement plan, not a blank page.
  2. Prove and baseline in the POC (8–12 weeks)The cost baseline — team rosters, rate card, compute billing — is measured with the client and agreed in writing, and savings are proven against it on one real use case. No baseline, no fee. 5×5 and POC fees are credited against deployment.
  3. Deploy and evidence continuouslyQBricks produces before/after files for every change and platform telemetry for every run. Compute savings are read from the platform's own billing; people savings against the baselined roster.
  4. Certify quarterly, invoice in arrearsSavings are certified against the ledger each quarter; the gain-share fee is invoiced only on certified amounts. If savings stop, fees stop.

Why the share rises (20% → 25% → 30%)

The ascending schedule is deliberately client-friendly at the start: in Year 1 — when the client carries adoption effort and the savings are newest — the bank keeps 80% of every euro. The share rises only as savings become proven, embedded run-rate. It also gives both sides a clean Year-3 decision point: extend at 30% in perpetuity, or part ways with the client keeping 100% of an established saving. One-off events (e.g. a single data migration) are priced once, at the Year-1 rate.

Glossary

Audited saving — a cost reduction versus the agreed baseline, evidenced by telemetry or roster and certified quarterly.
Baseline — the client-measured cost position (people, rates, compute) agreed in the POC before deployment.
Gain-share — Infinium's fee as an agreed % of audited savings; no licence-style fixed fee in this schedule.
Run-rate — the annualised saving once fully realised; the basis of the Year-4+ extension.
DBU — Databricks Unit, the platform's per-hour compute billing unit; cloud VMs are billed separately.
Credit — Snowflake's compute billing unit; dollar value depends on edition, region and cloud.
CU — Fabric Capacity Unit; an F-SKU is a fixed pool of CUs shared by all workloads.
Incremental processing — QBricks' DBSP engine processes only what changed, eliminating full reloads — the source of the compute reductions.
Before/after files — the per-change evidence pair QBricks retains, making every transformation auditable.
ODCS — Open Data Contract Standard; the contract QBricks enforces on every record in motion.

Commercial model

What exactly does the client pay, and when?
Only the gain-share percentage of certified savings, invoiced quarterly in arrears. There is no fixed fee in this schedule. The 5-day 5×5 and 8–12 week POC carry their own fixed prices, and both are credited against deployment.
What if the savings don't materialise?
Then there is nothing to share and no fee is due. The model is deliberately asymmetric: Infinium carries delivery risk, the client's downside is bounded at the 5×5/POC fees. That asymmetry is also why the baseline must be measured honestly — it protects both sides.
Why does Infinium's share rise over time rather than fall?
Because the client's effort is front-loaded and the proof is back-loaded. In Year 1 the bank keeps 80% while adoption is hardest; by Year 3 the saving is established run-rate and the 30% share reflects a proven outcome. It also creates a clean decision point at the end of Year 3.
What does "30% in perpetuity" actually mean?
If the client chooses to extend beyond Year 3, the share stays at 30% of the continuing audited savings for as long as the arrangement runs. It is an option, not an obligation: decline the extension and the client keeps 100% of the run-rate, with QBricks licensing (if any) handled separately.
Can the percentages be negotiated?
Yes — that is what this tool is for. The scenario buttons (Standard / Push-back / Premium) and the sliders reprice the whole deal instantly, including what each point of share is worth to the client in ROI terms.
How are one-off events like a migration priced?
A single event is priced once, at the Year-1 rate (20%): one event, one audited saving, one fee. Each further event is priced the same way. Events never progress up the multi-year schedule because there is no multi-year tail.
Is there a cap on Infinium's annual fee?
Optionally, yes — many gain-share contracts carry an annual fee cap, set in the Order Form. Savings above the cap revert 100% to the client, which protects the bank's budget envelope while keeping Infinium fully incentivised up to the ceiling. The Deal mechanics panel models it: set a cap and the combined view, ROI and NPV reprice (the cap is applied at combined level, allocated pro-rata across cards).
Are the figures nominal or discounted?
The card and combined tables are nominal — that is how savings are certified and invoiced. The combined panel also shows NPV of the client's retained savings and of Infinium's fees at an editable discount rate (default 8%), over both the 3-year term and a 5-year view including two extension years, so treasury can read the deal in present-value terms.

Evidence & audit

Who certifies the savings, and against what?
Savings are certified quarterly against the baseline agreed in the POC. People savings are evidenced against the baselined roster and rate card; compute savings are read from the platform's own billing exports; every data change carries QBricks before/after files. The certification pack is the client's — auditable by their internal audit or external assurance.
What stops the baseline being inflated to manufacture savings?
The baseline is measured jointly, from the client's own systems (rosters, rate cards, billing exports), documented in the POC, and signed by both parties before deployment. It can be re-opened by either side if scope changes materially.
If an FTE is redeployed rather than removed, is that a saving?
That depends on the baseline definition the client chooses, and it is agreed up front. Most banks count redeployment to funded vacancies as a cashable saving and redeployment to new discretionary work as a productivity gain; the ledger records which is which. The model assumes no severance costs either way.
What if the platform vendor changes its prices mid-term?
Compute baselines are normalised at the unit prices agreed in the POC, so a vendor price rise doesn't inflate QBricks' "savings" and a price cut doesn't erase them. A recalibration clause adjusts unit prices annually.
Could the same euro be counted in two cards?
No — each card has its own ledger against a distinct cost line: the remediation roster, the pipeline engineering roster, dev/test billing, and run billing plus the support roster. A person or a billing line can appear in only one ledger, checked at certification.

Scope & boundaries

What does "per business area and use case" mean in practice?
Each arrangement attaches to one named business area and use case — e.g. Transaction Monitoring — with its own baseline, ledger and schedule. Expanding to a second use case (KYC refresh, market data, regulatory reporting…) is a new schedule under the same MSA, so success compounds without renegotiating the framework.
Which costs are excluded from the model?
Storage, networking, software licences and severance are excluded throughout. The compute benchmarks also exclude egress and AI add-ons. Exclusions are listed in the Order Form so there are no surprises at certification.
Where do the compute benchmark prices come from?
Public list-price anchors as at June 2026: Databricks ~$0.15/DBU (jobs) to ~$0.55/DBU (all-purpose, Premium) with cloud VMs billed separately; Snowflake credits ~$2–4 by edition and region; Fabric ~$0.18/CU-hour PAYG with ~40% off on 1-year reserved; BigQuery Editions $0.04–0.10/slot-hour with ~20–40% commitment discounts (on-demand $6.25/TiB). Every figure is editable because real prices depend on the client's enterprise agreement — the benchmarks frame the conversation, the client's bill provides the baseline.
Does QBricks itself add compute cost?
QBricks operates on metadata only and runs on the client's infrastructure; its footprint is small relative to the reloads it removes and is netted in the measured saving — the certified number is net of QBricks' own consumption.

Contract & governance

What is the contractual vehicle?
An MSA with an Order Form per business area and use case carrying the baseline, the savings ledger definition, the schedule and the extension clause. Governing law follows the QBricks framework (Netherlands law).
How does gain-share interact with QBricks platform licences?
This schedule is pure gain-share. Where a deployment also carries a Core Platform licence under the pricing grid, the Order Form states it explicitly and the client ROI is presented net of both — the tool can be read either way, and the certification ledger separates the two.
Can the client exit?
Yes — at the Year-3 decision point without penalty, and earlier per the MSA's termination provisions. Fees are only ever due on savings already certified; there is no clawback of the client's retained share.
What does the client have to do to make the savings real?
Three things, all in the transition plan: provide the baseline data honestly in the POC, action the people-side decisions (redeployment) on the agreed timetable, and decommission the legacy pipelines and jobs once cutover is verified — savings only certify when the old cost actually stops.

Using this tool

Can I save a negotiation position and come back to it?
Yes — Export writes every input (schedule, cap, discount rate, all drivers and benchmark settings) to a JSON file; Import restores it. Use it to snapshot a client's counter-offer mid-meeting, or to share a position with a colleague. Nothing is sent anywhere; the file is local.
How do I leave a copy with the client?
Print produces a clean one-page summary of the Calculator view (inputs panel hidden) — use the browser's "Save as PDF". Copy summary puts a plain-text deal summary including NPV on the clipboard for an email or meeting note.
